Phase 1 · Planning

Is a Scoping Review the Right Approach?

Confirm this is the best review type for your goals before investing months of work.

What a Scoping Review Does

A scoping review maps the breadth, nature, and characteristics of evidence on a topic. It identifies key concepts, evidence types, and research gaps — without typically assessing study quality or pooling quantitative results.

Choose a Scoping Review When You Want To

Map the landscape

Chart what exists on a broad, emerging, or interdisciplinary topic.

Clarify definitions

Investigate how key terms are used across disciplines.

Identify gaps

Find underexplored populations, methods, or settings.

Precursor to systematic review

Determine if a full systematic review is feasible.

✍️ Your Decision

This rationale becomes part of your protocol's background section.
  • Choosing a scoping review to avoid quality appraisal. If your real question is about effectiveness, a systematic review may be more appropriate — and reviewers will notice.
  • Underestimating scope. Scoping reviews are not "easier" than systematic reviews. They often cover more sources across more diverse evidence types.
  • Starting without a team. You need at least two independent reviewers for screening. Identify your team before beginning.

Developing Your Research Question

Use the PCC framework to frame a broad yet focused inquiry.

The PCC Framework

Scoping reviews use PCC — Population, Concept, Context — rather than PICO. This accommodates broader, more exploratory questions without requiring pre-specified interventions or outcomes.

ConceptCulturally responsive pedagogy — how it is defined, implemented, and assessed
ContextK-12 settings in North America, 2010–present

💡 Iteration Is Expected

Your question will evolve as you learn the literature. Arksey & O'Malley note this process is iterative — refining your PCC after preliminary searching is normal.

  • Question too broad. "What is known about mental health?" will return thousands of irrelevant results. Every PCC element should meaningfully narrow scope.
  • Question too narrow. If you can only find 5–10 sources in a preliminary search, your question may be better suited to a focused systematic review.
  • Conflating PCC with PICO. Scoping reviews don't need a specific intervention or comparator. If you find yourself writing those in, reconsider your review type.
  • Skipping preliminary searching. Run test searches in 1–2 databases before finalizing your PCC to check that the volume and relevance are manageable.
Phase 1 · Planning

Defining Eligibility Criteria

Decide what's in and what's out — before you start screening.

Why This Matters

Clear criteria ensure multiple reviewers apply the same standards. They should flow directly from your PCC elements.

Key Decisions for SSH Reviews

Source Types

Scoping reviews can include peer-reviewed articles, grey literature, policy docs, theses, and more. Define which and why.

Date Range

Narrow for emerging topics; broader for conceptual mapping.

Language

English-only is common but should be noted as a limitation.

Study Design

Scoping reviews typically welcome all methodologies: quantitative, qualitative, mixed, theoretical.

  • Vague criteria. "Relevant studies" is not a criterion. Each element should be testable — could two reviewers independently apply it and agree?
  • Excluding grey literature without justification. Scoping reviews benefit from broad source types. If you limit to peer-reviewed only, acknowledge this as a limitation.
  • Forgetting language limits. English-only is practical but introduces bias. State it explicitly in your protocol.
  • Not aligning criteria to PCC. Every inclusion criterion should map to a PCC element. If it doesn't, reconsider whether it belongs.

Managing Citations with Zotero or EndNote

A citation manager is essential for organizing your search results, deduplicating across databases, and exporting citations to screening tools. Start using one before you run your first search.

Zotero (Free, Open Source)

Works across platforms with browser integration for one-click saving. Strong group library features for team reviews. Exports to RIS, CSV, and BibTeX for import into Covidence/Rayyan.

Download Zotero →

Tip: Create a dedicated collection for your review, with sub-collections for each database search. Use tags to track screening status if not using Covidence.

EndNote (Licensed)

Powerful deduplication features — especially useful when merging results from many databases. Handles large libraries well. Direct export to Covidence and other review tools.

Available through Tulane →

Tip: Use EndNote's "Find Duplicates" before exporting to Covidence for an initial dedup pass. Covidence will catch remaining duplicates automatically.

Citation Manager Workflow for Scoping Reviews

1. Run your search in each database → 2. Export results (.RIS format preferred) → 3. Import into Zotero/EndNote, organizing by database → 4. Run initial deduplication → 5. Export combined, deduplicated set → 6. Import into Covidence or Rayyan for screening

Keep your citation manager library as a permanent archive of all search results. Even after screening, you may need to return to excluded citations if your criteria change.

✍️ Describe Your Screening Plan

How many reviewers? What tool? Pilot screening plan? Conflict resolution?
  • Single reviewer screening. This is the most common methodological weakness cited by peer reviewers. Always use at least two independent screeners.
  • Not piloting. Screening the first 25–50 titles together calibrates your team and catches criteria ambiguities early — before you've screened hundreds.
  • Not recording full-text exclusion reasons. These are required for the PRISMA flow diagram. Track them during screening, not retroactively.
  • Being too liberal or conservative. When uncertain at title/abstract stage, include it — you can exclude at full text with a documented reason.
Phase 3 · Analysis

Data Charting (Extraction)

Record key information from each included source into a standardized form.

Charting, Not Extracting

The goal is to map the evidence landscape, not extract data for statistical pooling. Pilot your charting form on 3–5 sources and refine iteratively.

✍️ Design Your Charting Form

List each variable. What will your results tables look like?
How will you pilot and refine the form?
  • Too many variables. Chart what you need to answer your question. Every additional variable multiplies extraction time across all sources.
  • Not piloting the form. Test on 3–5 diverse sources before full extraction. You'll discover ambiguous variables immediately.
  • Extracting outcomes like a systematic review. In scoping reviews, you're mapping the landscape — chart how concepts are defined and studied, not effect sizes.
  • Inconsistent charting between reviewers. Create clear operational definitions for each variable. "Study design" means different things to different people.
Phase 3 · Analysis

Synthesis & Reporting

Make sense of the landscape you've mapped and present it clearly.

Two Complementary Approaches

📊 Numerical Summary

Tables and charts: distribution by year, country, methodology, population, discipline.

📝 Thematic Synthesis

Key themes, patterns, and gaps. Goes beyond counting to interpret the evidence.

✍️ Plan Your Synthesis

Describe numerical summaries, thematic analysis, and any visualizations.
Will you consult practitioners, policymakers, or community members? If so, how and when?
  • Only presenting numbers. Tables and charts are necessary but not sufficient. Thematic narrative synthesis is where you interpret what the evidence means collectively.
  • Treating it like a systematic review. Don't calculate effect sizes or make effectiveness claims. Scoping reviews map; they don't pool.
  • Forgetting the PRISMA flow diagram. This is required, not optional. Covidence generates one automatically from your screening data.
  • Not linking back to objectives. Your synthesis should directly answer the questions stated in your protocol. Every table and theme should connect to a PCC element.

Phase 4 · Writing

PRISMA-ScR Reporting Checklist

Track your reporting completeness — 20 essential and 2 optional items.

About PRISMA-ScR

The PRISMA-ScR checklist (Tricco et al., 2018) provides minimum reporting items for scoping reviews. Journals increasingly require a completed checklist with submission. Use this tracker as you write.

0 of 22 items addressed
  • 1. Title — Identify as a scoping review
  • 2. Structured summary — Background, objectives, methods, results, conclusions
  • 3. Rationale — Context of what is already known
  • 4. Objectives — Explicit objectives and review question(s)
  • 5. Protocol & registration — Protocol location, registration info
  • 6. Eligibility criteria — Inclusion/exclusion specifications
  • 7. Information sources — All sources searched, date of last search
  • 8. Search — Full strategy for at least one database
  • 9. Selection of sources — Process for selecting sources
  • 10. Data charting — Methods of charting, any piloting
  • 11. Data items — All variables sought
  • 12. Critical appraisal — Methods, if done optional
  • 13. Synthesis methods — How data were handled/summarized
  • 14. Source selection results — Numbers at each stage + flow diagram
  • 15. Source characteristics — Characteristics of each source
  • 16. Appraisal results — If done optional
  • 17. Individual results — Charted data for each source
  • 18. Synthesis of results — Main findings summarized
  • 19. Summary of evidence — Link to objectives
  • 20. Limitations — Review process limitations
  • 21. Conclusions — Interpretation and implications
  • 22. Funding — Sources and funder roles
